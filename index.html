The Quiet Life vs.
The Ambitious Life

Talha Hassan
February 2025

I've always struggled with the tension between ambition and peace.

For most of my 20s, I felt pulled toward a big, impressive life — high achievement, bigger roles, global opportunities, and the feeling of becoming someone extraordinary. That ambition felt exciting, but it also made me restless.

I always felt like I was supposed to be somewhere else, doing something "bigger," chasing something just out of reach. At the same time, there has always been another part of me — the part that wants a quiet life. Time with my family. A calm home. A simple spiritual routine. Work that doesn't consume me. A pace that lets me actually experience my life instead of sprinting through it.

For years, it felt like I had to choose. Ambition or peace. Impact or presence. Movement or stillness. And because of that, I constantly felt like I was failing at one or the other. Choosing peace felt like abandoning my potential. Choosing ambition felt like abandoning myself.

Recently, I've started to see this tension differently. The quiet life and the ambitious life aren't enemies. They don't cancel each other out. They're just designed to do different jobs.

The ambitious life sets the direction — the goals I care about, the work I want to do, the impact I want to have, the opportunities I want to grow into. The quiet life sets the pace — the way I move toward those goals without burning myself out, losing who I am, or sacrificing the people and values that matter most.

It's not about picking one forever. It's about letting each version of me lead when it's supposed to. When I'm building, I let ambition take the wheel. When I'm living, I let peace slow everything down.

I no longer see it as a battle between two identities. It's just one identity learning to live with intention.

In the end, I've chosen the quiet life — not instead of the ambitious one, but as the foundation that makes ambition sustainable.

A quiet life isn't a smaller life. It's just a better pace for the person I want to become.

AI as a
Second Brain

Talha Hassan
March 2026

I used to think productivity meant speed. Get through more things, faster, in less time. If I was busy, I was doing well.

That's not how I see it anymore.

About a year ago I started using AI seriously. Claude, mostly. At first it was just the small stuff. Cleaning up an email I'd already written in my head. Pulling together notes before a meeting. Reformatting a slide deck so I didn't have to spend forty minutes dragging boxes around in PowerPoint.

None of that sounds like a big deal. But all of it used to cost me something. Every time I switched from real work to busywork, it took me ten or fifteen minutes to get my head back into what I was actually doing. That adds up. You don't notice how fragmented your day is until the fragments go away.

Once the small stuff was handled, I started leaning on it for bigger things. Stress-testing the logic in a financial model before presenting it. Structuring how I wanted to lay out a recommendation. Building an entire system to prep for job interviews, where I could track my stories, log questions I'd been asked, and review what landed and what didn't. I built this website with it. Not by saying "make me a website" but by working through it together, back and forth, the same way you'd work with someone sharp who happened to know how to code.

That's the thing most people get wrong about AI. It doesn't think for you. It handles the stuff that gets in the way of your thinking. The judgment is still mine. The strategy is still mine. But the hours I used to lose to formatting and context switching and rewriting the same email three times? Those hours come back to you. And you feel it. I'm more focused at work now than I've been in years. Not because I'm doing more. Because more of what I'm doing actually matters.

Some people think it's a shortcut or that it makes you lazy. I get that. But honestly I've found the opposite. When you take away the busywork, you're left with the hard stuff. The actual thinking. That's where you grow, and there's no way to automate that.

Here's what I think most companies are missing. They're treating AI like an IT project. A tool rollout, a policy update, maybe a lunch and learn. But the people who are actually getting value from it right now didn't wait for any of that. They just started using it because they were curious. The real change won't come from the top. It'll come from people inside the company who are already doing it and can show everyone else what it looks like in practice. Cultural sponsors, not software rollouts.

I work in energy. It's an industry that moves carefully, and for good reason. The assets are long-lived, the stakes are real, and people value caution. But there's a difference between being careful about how you adopt something and refusing to adopt it at all. The companies that figure this out will get a little faster, a little sharper, a little more focused every year. And that compounds. The ones that don't will just keep wondering why their teams feel so stretched.

I'm not an AI evangelist. I'm an analyst who found a better way to work. The second brain isn't about doing less. It's about making sure you spend your time on the things that actually need you.

That's not laziness. That's leverage.

How I Learn the
Unconventional Way

Talha Hassan
March 2026

When I start something new, I don't start at the beginning. I start with the hardest problem I can find.

Most people learn by building up. Start with the basics, get comfortable, take on more responsibility gradually. That makes sense and I understand why people do it that way. But it's never been how I work best.

Every time I've moved into a new role, the first thing I ask is "what's the biggest unsolved problem on this team right now?" Not because I think I'll solve it on day one. But because trying to solve it is the fastest way to learn everything that matters.

When you start with the hardest problem, you're forced to understand the full picture immediately. You can't fake it. You have to learn the systems, the data, the stakeholders, the history of why it hasn't been solved yet. You have to ask questions that might seem basic. You have to admit what you don't know. And all of that learning happens with context, because you're not studying the business in the abstract. You're studying it through the lens of something real that needs to get done.

When I joined a new team focused on carbon compliance, I could have spent the first few months learning the regulations, reading old reports, sitting in on meetings. Instead I asked what the biggest gap was. The answer was that the existing carbon model couldn't handle the current asset base or the evolving regulatory framework. So I rebuilt it from scratch. That single project forced me to learn the volume forecasts across every facility, how emissions intensity was calculated, how credit markets worked, how the regulatory timelines interacted with our commercial planning. I learned more in those first few months than I would have in a year of onboarding the traditional way.

The same thing happened when I started doing optimization work on gas processing facilities. Rather than slowly familiarizing myself with each plant's operations, I went straight to the cost structure and asked where the biggest inefficiencies were. That's how I found a facility running an onsite electricity turbine that was costing millions more per year than grid power. I wouldn't have found that by easing into the role. I found it because I started with the hardest question.

It's not just a work thing either. In my MBA classes I do the same thing. When I'm learning a new concept, I don't start with the introductory questions. I go straight to the hardest problem set I can find and try to work through it. I'll get stuck. I'll get things wrong. But by the time I've fought through it, I understand the concept at a level that working up from the basics would have taken me three times as long to reach.

I think the reason this works is that hard problems are connected to everything. They sit at the intersection of multiple systems, multiple teams, multiple assumptions. When you pull on one, you end up learning the whole organization. Easy problems teach you a task. Hard problems teach you the business.

I won't pretend this approach is comfortable. It means being the person in the room who knows the least about a topic and is still trying to lead the work on it. It means being wrong sometimes and having to adjust quickly. But I've found that people respect it. Teams appreciate someone who shows up and says "what's the thing that's been stuck?" rather than "walk me through the org chart."

I'm not saying this is how everyone should learn. But it's how I learn. And it's why I'm not worried about walking into something new. I've done it before and the pattern is always the same. Find the hardest problem. Start pulling on it. Let everything else follow.

Keyera Energy Corp. · 2024

About

I grew up wanting to understand how things work. That inclination took me into engineering, which taught me to be precise, to respect complexity, and to distrust elegant-sounding explanations that don't survive contact with the numbers.

I work in energy, and the more time I spend in it, the more I think it's one of the most interesting industries to try to understand. It's never just about pipelines or power grids — it's about how countries relate to each other, how policy shapes investment over decades, how the physical world and the political world are more entangled than most people realize. I find that genuinely fascinating.

Outside of work, I think about career and life more broadly — what it means to build something that lasts, how to stay curious without becoming scattered, and how to be ambitious without losing the things that made the ambition worth it. I became a father recently, which has sharpened almost everything I thought I knew.

I read a lot. Morgan Housel is the writer I return to most. I try to write the way he does — clearly, without pretending the world is simpler than it is.

This site exists because writing forces better thinking. Everything here is an honest attempt at that.
